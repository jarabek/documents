
## What I Expect from My Team

### Candor

If you feel I'm not understanding your needs correctly, not clearing roadblocks, not helping you grow in the direction you want, I want honest and direct feedback. If I don't hear it, it's going to take me much longer to realize I'm not investing in you effectively.

As it relates to your accountabilities: I'd rather hear about problems too soon than too late. If you've identified a problem in the code or the process, don't sit on it. I can't promise I'll make it an immediate priority, but I can promise I'll never be mad about receiving bad news.

Bringing a problem also means coming with at least one proposed solution.

Everyone makes mistakes. That's fine. I just want to see learning happen.

Candor does not mean saying whatever you want without consequence. It means communicating with directness *and* empathy.

---

### Autonomy

Autonomy is not cutting you loose to figure things out alone. It means developing mutual trust. We often make decisions with incomplete information. I want to trust that, given the best available information and a clear direction, you will make sensible decisions. I want you to trust that I won't be disappointed or angry about a decision you made in good faith. If you are unsure and need input, please ask me for input.

---

### Mastery

You're here because you're highly trained and talented at your craft. I will work with you to set clear competency expectations, and I expect you to engage in continual learning as part of your job. There is budget for training. Let me know what would help and I'll try to make it happen.

---

### Communication

We are building highly sophisticated products. Your ability to communicate effectively with the people around you is critically important. Communicate issues early. If something you are accountable for looks like it's going off track, say something to your team and to me. I won't be upset if something is off track. But my knowing early is what lets me set accurate expectations for the business.

---

### Engagement

Every person on the team has a stake in what we are creating. I don't want anyone keeping their head down and punching the clock. That means having opinions about what we're building and sharing them. It means stepping up to help teammates when they need it, and not letting them flounder. The product's quality, stability, and reliability are a direct reflection of the team we are.

---

### Avoid Heroics

Working late or on weekends happens sometimes, but it should be rare. These situations should not be celebrated or seen as an opportunity to distinguish yourself. They should be seen as a failure in planning or process. When they happen, we should ask why, and how we avoid it next time.

We avoid heroics by collaboratively managing commitments, pushing back when the team is being over-committed, and communicating risks early.

--- 

I can provide opportunities to expose you to new problems, business domains, and roles, but **knowing what you want is your responsibility.**

---

## What My Team Can Expect from Me

- I will do everything in my power to remove organizational impediments from you doing your best work. (You should still try removing those roadblocks yourself first.)
- You will get regular feedback, both positive and constructive. I will tell you when you are doing well and when you need to improve, and how we can work on it together. Constructive feedback is not criticism; it is an investment in your growth as a professional.
- I will provide a clear job description and career ladder. I will help you understand what paths exist and what each one requires.
- We will work together to define career goals and develop a plan to help you achieve them.
- I will be as transparent as possible about company goals, product direction, and any decisions that impact the team.
- I will try to surprise you as rarely as possible.
